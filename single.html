Mindful Self-Care: Your Internal and External Environments

Part 3 of the Self-Care Series for Mental Health Professionals

3 CE Hours

Learning Objectives

Upon completing this course, mental health professionals will be able to:

  • Explain the concept of mindfulness and its application to psychotherapist self-care
  • Identify and practice multiple mindfulness-related techniques including meditation, body scans, and centering exercises
  • Describe the research evidence supporting mindfulness training for mental health professionals
  • Apply self-compassion practices to reduce self-criticism and enhance professional resilience
  • Implement gratitude exercises to increase positive emotions and well-being
  • Recognize the importance of basic biobehavioral self-care: sleep, rest, nutrition, exercise, and human contact
  • Assess and optimize the physical work environment to support therapeutic effectiveness
  • Identify organizational and systemic factors that contribute to or detract from clinician self-care
  • Develop strategies for environmental control to enhance practice sustainability

Introduction

Mindfulness is of emerging prominence in psychotherapy, and its supportive research demands its own use by and for the psychotherapist. In this course, we canvass for self-care consideration a bevy of mindfulness-related practices: taking a moment, practicing mindfulness, using meditative anchors, adding meditation, cultivating self-compassion, expressing gratitude, and honoring transitions. It is way more than taking yoga classes on the weekend.

The course then minds the body of the practitioner by addressing sleep, rest, nutrition, exercise, and human contact. Finally, we move to minding the external environment of the mental health practice, including the physical environment, sensory awareness, work safety, business support, and systems of self-care. The course features pan-theoretical methods of self-care, highlights representative research, infuses examples from master therapists, and concludes with a practical self-care checklist.

Part 1: Minding the Mind - Mindfulness Practices

What is Mindfulness?

"It is better to conquer yourself than to win a thousand battles. Then the victory is yours. It cannot be taken from you."
– Buddha

Mindfulness integrates several self-care strategies: It minds the body, sets boundaries between your client focus and your self-care, restructures your foraging squirrel or monkey brain thoughts, and represents at least a momentary escape from your usual professional activity.

In the simplest of terms, mindfulness means paying attention in a particular way: purposefully, in the present moment, and nonjudgmentally (Kabat-Zinn, 1990). As self-care, mindfulness helps us to sense and experience subtler aspects of emotion in the daily process of living. It increases a gentle awareness of affects, thoughts, and ways of responding to stress and strain – as well as pleasure.

Taking a Moment

The life of a mental health professional generally proves hectic and complex. The challenge is how to remain balanced throughout the day and how to effectively do what needs to be done next, while actually enjoying the day.

Take a mindful moment before you start the day. Even a two-minute pause helps. A brief centering exercise may suffice: sitting quietly in the office for 30 seconds, breathing calmly and deeply, clearing your mind of things that need to done later, and focusing on the here and now – and then on the patient you are about to see.

Using Meditative Anchors

Our frenzied monkey brains frequently require something physical and concrete to anchor meditative practice. The process of breathing constitutes the most prevalent anchor. Focus and experience how you breathe. Just attend, just experience. Do not judge it, or analyze patterns, or control it. Just breathe and attend to the breathing process.

Cultivating Self-Compassion

Self-compassion proves essential when reflecting on our clinical mistakes, empathic failures, and painful life experiences. Self-compassion consists of three interrelated elements: kindness, a sense of common humanity, and mindfulness. Training and practice in self-compassion prove helpful in mitigating our uber-critical thoughts and in developing inner resources to successfully engage difficulties in daily life.

Expressing Gratitude

Gratitude exercises are among the best-documented and researched successes in positive psychology. One classic exercise is "three good things in life" – generating a list of three good things about one's life experienced recently. The results are modest but meaningful decreases in negative affect and increases in well-being.

Part 2: Minding the Body

"The human body is the best picture of the human soul."
– Ludwig Wittgenstein

Psychotherapists are so intent and focused on sophisticated self-care methods that we frequently overlook the biobehavioral basics of self-care: adequate sleep, rest, nutrition, exercise, and human contact. Let's not neglect the fundamentals of self-care.

Sleep and Rest

In our interviews with master practitioners, a recurrent self-care theme is obtaining ample sleep – a powerful solution to occupational distress. Our watchword as psychotherapists should be "Mens sana in corpore sano" (a healthy mind in a healthy body). A nightly investment in sufficient rest leads to greater resilience and accomplishment.

Nutrition and Hydration

The self-care imperative is to eat balanced nutritious meals and to hydrate adequately during the day. Monitor your fluid intake during the workday. Aim for drinking half your body weight in ounces daily; for example, if you weigh 150 pounds, your daily target would be about 75 ounces.

Exercise

Surveys involving thousands of psychotherapists discover that 71%-89% engage in regular physical exercise. Associations between exercise and well-being have been documented repeatedly for decades. Exercise most positively impacts the therapist's physical stamina, emotional mood, and mental stamina.

Human Contact

Contact comfort is of overwhelming importance in the development of affectional response. We all need contact comfort, a little cuddling. Some clinicians keep a favorite pillow or afghan nearby during sessions, to hold or touch. Others allow for time for phone calls to close friends and family members in the midst of a busy day.

Part 3: Minding the Environment

"I don't want to be a product of my environment. I want my environment to be a product of me."
– William Monahan

Most recommendations to promote psychotherapist self-care fall into the category of changing the person. This is essential but incomplete. We need to comprehensively address the person of the psychotherapist, the work environment, and their interaction.

The Physical Environment

Let's begin with the tangible: the physical environment in which you work. Research has identified several features of psychotherapy offices that support treatment objectives. Perceived quality of care and comfort improve with an increase in office softness, personalization, and orderliness.

Sensory Awareness

We can cultivate our sensory awareness. Sensory diversion can help temporarily mollify nearly all forms of painful affect and replenish our overtaxed senses. Behold your surroundings using vision, hearing, touch, gustation, and olfaction.

Work Safety

Evidence is accumulating about the personal safety of psychotherapists. More than 80% of psychotherapists feared that a client would physically attack them and about 20% had actually been attacked by a client. Your self-care demands a comfortable and safe practice environment.

Business Support

One way to help therapists differentiate themselves from their practices is to imagine the practice as a distinct entity, something apart from themselves. Hire office assistance for tasks you dislike, learn to streamline your office practices, or hire a business coach for cost-effective approaches.

Systems of Self-Care

Self-care needs to be addressed at multiple levels: the individual practitioner, the physical environment, the organizational context, and the larger systems of the mental health professions. Only a multifaceted systems approach will suffice.
